    Solving Problems with Experience

    In the tech industry, experience is more than just knowing syntax or frameworks—it's about solving real-world challenges efficiently. The ability to connect technologies, apply logical reasoning, and make strategic decisions under pressure often separates a capable developer from an expert.

    My problem-solving approach always begins with a clear understanding of the root issue. I break down challenges into structured, manageable steps, then deliver scalable, secure, and optimized solutions. For instance, when facing a critical production system failure, rather than patching blindly, I analyze logs, replicate the issue, and isolate the root cause—minimizing downtime and avoiding regression.

    Experience is not about memorizing every method—it's about knowing where and how to find the right answers quickly.

    A Perspective on Hiring & Interviews

    One of the most common flaws I've noticed in technical hiring processes is the tendency to test memory over mindset. Far too often, interviews focus on trivia-like questions or unrelated theoretical scenarios, instead of assessing how a candidate solves real-world problems.

    From my experience—especially having served in top-level managerial roles—interviewing should be more about evaluating how someone thinks, collaborates, and approaches complexity. Respect for experience and professionalism should guide these interactions. If someone has years of practical experience and holds advanced qualifications, it's reasonable to expect a certain level of courtesy and thoughtful dialogue.

    Let's be honest—asking a senior developer to write a bubble sort or explain what SOLID stands for as a measure of their capability is not just outdated—it borders on insulting. Instead, focus on evaluating their ability to make architectural decisions, lead teams, and solve business-critical issues. Technical questions should be relevant, meaningful, and aligned with the candidate’s role and seniority.

    In short, intelligence doesn't mean proving you're smarter than your candidate. It means knowing when to listen, what to ask, and how to measure potential where it matters most—in real work.
